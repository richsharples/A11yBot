A11yBot · Direction B · Professional blue

One language.
Ink and a single blue.

Dropping the phosphor brand simplifies the system rather than complicates it. The audit findings still hold — color sprawl, button vocabulary, type chaos — but the answer collapses to a single working surface with one blue accent, a calibrated semantic palette, and a redrawn robot mark that keeps the personality without the CRT theatrics.

01
Does the recommendation change?

Mostly it gets simpler.

Still true from the audit

  • ✓The color sprawl is the headline visual problem.
  • ✓Button vocabulary has at least seven hand-tuned styles that should consolidate to four.
  • ✓Type sizes are ad-hoc — commit to a 6-step scale.
  • ✓Badges and chips should split into three categories: conformance level, evidence density, neutral metadata.
  • ✓The 3-pane review IA, the workflow, the keyboard model all work. Don't touch them.
  • ✓The "New project" modal is too theatrical — destructive confirmation should feel grave, not styled.

What changes by going blue

  • →No more "two zones." The whole product is one paper surface.
  • →The contrast risk in the header largely evaporates — no more low-alpha-on-dark.
  • →Save / confirm becomes blue (the primary CTA color), not green. Green goes back to meaning "PM confirmed" only.
  • →The status log loses the CRT mood and becomes a quiet inline strip — fine, because the brand isn't carrying it anymore.
  • →The logo redraws. Same robot character, geometric line art, no scanline texture or glow.
  • →Surface shifts from warm paper to cool near-white. Better for procurement / institutional contexts.

One rule about blue: the brand navy #0B1B33 is used for the wordmark and as the deepest ink. The bright accent #1F6FEB is reserved for: primary buttons, links, the focus ring, active selection, and progress fills. Nothing else. If a label is just informational, it's ink — not accent. This is the discipline that keeps the screen calm.

03
Mark direction

Same robot, fewer pretensions.

The CRT robot worked as a brand idea — it's literally personable for an automated tool. Carry the personality forward, but redraw it as a precise geometric mark: square head, square eyes, optional antenna. Three weights below show how it can flex across contexts.

A · Outline

Lightweight mark for headers, docs, favicons. Renders cleanly at 16px.

B · Solid with accent eyes

Default app icon. Navy body, accent-blue eyes signal "active / on-line." This is the canonical mark.

C · Reversed

For dark backgrounds — splash, login, the GitHub README hero. Solid white on navy.

Fig. 1 — Review screen, Professional Blue. Same workflow, one design language.

Single canvas, calibrated accents

Everything on one near-white surface. Selection is accent-soft (#E8EFFC); the active criterion is highlighted by its left rail and white card. The eye finds where it is instantly.

Blue is reserved

Accent blue appears in exactly four places: the primary CTA, the progress bar, the active tab underline, and the interview badge. Every other tone is ink. This is what makes the screen feel quiet despite the density.

Status returns to its station

The CRT mood is gone, so the status log becomes a slim inline strip — visible when needed, doesn't shout. The OK dot replaces the running-text status badge family.

06
A note on dark mode

You can still have one — just not by accident.

If a dark variant is wanted later, build it deliberately on the same token graph: brand navy becomes a deep slate (#0E1626), surface becomes near-black, accent stays bright blue. The mark uses the reversed (white) variant. The semantic palette flips its bg/fg pair. This is straightforward once the tokens exist — and it does not require resurrecting phosphor.

07
If you commit to this

Order of operations.

1

Swap globals.css tokens to the values in §02.

About 20 CSS variables. Update tailwind.config.ts to expose them. Everything downstream gets cheaper. Half a day.

2

Redraw the logo as inline SVG.

The mark in this doc is already vector — copy it into components/Logo.tsx with three exports: <Logo />, <LogoReversed />, <LogoMarkOnly />. Replace public/a11ybot-logo.png usages. Add a favicon.svg using the same source. One day.

3

Build four React button variants.

Button.Primary, Button.Secondary, Button.Ghost, Button.Danger. Remove every inline button className from the three review components. Save / confirm uses Primary (blue), not green. Half a day.

4

Refactor evidence/level into three components.

<Chip level="..." />, <Badge variant="..." />, <Banner variant="..." />. Use them in CriterionDetail, CriteriaReview sidebar, and the chapter list. Half a day.

5

Rebuild the header and status bar in the new style.

Header gets the tab spine, single primary CTA, and labeled metadata. Status bar moves to the quiet inline pattern. Discard the CRT scanline texture and all text-[#39FF14]/N usage. One day.

6

Quiet the modal.

"Discard & start new" moves onto surface, gets a sober ink title and a text-only danger button. Should feel grave, not styled. Two hours.

Approximate total: three to four days of focused work. Step 1 alone delivers ~60% of the visible quality lift. Nothing here requires changing your stack, your routing, your component split, or your data model. This is paint and naming, not rebuild.
